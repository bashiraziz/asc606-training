# Module 3 — Step 1: Identify the contract

## Overview

Before any revenue can be recognized, an enforceable contract must exist. ASC 606-10-25-1 requires that **all five** of the following criteria be met:

| # | Criterion | Key question |
|---|---|---|
| (a) | Approval & commitment | Have all parties approved the contract and committed to perform? |
| (b) | Rights identified | Have each party's rights regarding the goods or services been identified? |
| (c) | Payment terms | Have the payment terms for the goods or services been identified? |
| (d) | Commercial substance | Will the risk, timing, or amount of cash flows change as a result of this contract? |
| (e) | Collectibility | Is it probable that the entity will collect substantially all of the consideration? |

:::danger If any criterion is not met
Do not recognize revenue. Record any consideration received as a **liability** (refund obligation or contract liability). Reassess when facts change — criteria may be met in a future period.
:::

## Criterion (a) — Approval and commitment

The contract must be approved in a form the entity recognizes — written, oral, or implied by customary business practice. Both parties must be committed to perform.

:::note Oral and implied contracts
A written contract is not required. An oral agreement or a pattern of conduct that creates legally enforceable rights and obligations qualifies. Document the basis for your conclusion.
:::

## Criterion (b) — Rights identified

The entity must be able to identify what goods or services it promises to transfer and what the customer's right to receive them is. This does not require exhaustive detail, but must be sufficient to support Step 2 analysis.

## Criterion (c) — Payment terms

The entity must be able to identify the amount, timing, and terms of payment. Variable pricing, usage-based fees, and contingent payments are acceptable — they are addressed in Step 3 (transaction price). The key is that a mechanism for determining consideration exists.

## Criterion (d) — Commercial substance

The exchange must have economic substance — the risk, timing, or amount of the entity's cash flows is expected to change as a result of the contract. This criterion prevents revenue recognition for circular cash flows (entity pays customer; customer pays entity back) with no economic content.

## Criterion (e) — Collectibility

Collectibility is assessed at the **portfolio level** or **individual contract level**. The threshold is *probable* — meaning likely to occur under ASC 606 (consistent with the "probable" threshold used elsewhere in U.S. GAAP, generally ≥75-80% likelihood).

:::warning Collectibility ≠ full contract price
You only need to assess collectibility for the amount of consideration to which you are entitled **after concessions**. If you routinely accept less than the stated contract price, assess collectibility on the realistic expected amount, not the stated price.
:::

## Contract modifications — three scenarios

A contract modification is a change in the scope or price of a contract that is approved by both parties (ASC 606-10-25-10).

There are three accounting scenarios:

### Scenario 1 — New separate contract

**Conditions:** The modification adds **distinct** goods or services AND the price for those goods/services reflects their stand-alone selling price.

**Accounting:** Account for the modification as a separate contract. The original contract continues unchanged.

**Example:** A SaaS company adds 5 additional user licenses to an existing subscription at the same per-user price as the original contract.

### Scenario 2 — Termination of original + new contract

**Conditions:** The remaining goods/services are distinct from those already transferred, but the modification **does not** add goods at standalone price.

**Accounting:** Treat the modification as a termination of the original contract and the beginning of a new one. The transaction price of the new contract = remaining consideration from the original + any new consideration from the modification.

**Example:** A construction company agrees to reduce the scope of a project mid-build for a price reduction. The remaining work is distinct but repriced.

### Scenario 3 — Cumulative catch-up adjustment

**Conditions:** The remaining goods/services are **not distinct** — they are part of a single performance obligation already partially satisfied.

**Accounting:** Update the estimate of total transaction price and progress, then record a cumulative catch-up (increase or decrease) in the current period.

**Example:** A long-term service contract where work already done is not separable from remaining work — scope change requires adjusting cumulative recognized revenue.

## Contract combinations

ASC 606-10-25-9: Two or more contracts entered into at or near the same time with the same customer should be combined and accounted for as a single contract if **any** of these criteria apply:

1. The contracts are negotiated as a package with a single commercial objective
2. The amount of consideration in one contract depends on the price or performance of the other
3. The goods or services promised in the contracts (or some goods or services) are a single performance obligation

:::tip Why this matters
Contract combinations prevent structuring abuse — breaking one deal into multiple contracts to achieve a different revenue outcome. If the economics suggest one deal, treat it as one deal.
:::

## Reassessment

The Step 1 analysis is not set-and-forget. Reassess when facts change — particularly collectibility (criterion e), which can deteriorate over the contract life.

When a previously recognized contract no longer meets the criteria:
- Stop recognizing revenue
- Reverse previously recognized revenue only if there is no basis to retain the consideration already received (i.e., if the entity has no further obligations and the customer cannot demand a refund)

## Key takeaways

- All five criteria must be met before any revenue is recognized — one failure stops the analysis
- Collectibility is assessed on the realistic expected amount, not the stated contract price
- Three modification scenarios: new contract / termination + new contract / catch-up adjustment — choose based on whether remaining goods are distinct AND whether the price reflects SSP
- Contract combinations prevent artificial splitting; combine when one deal is broken into multiple contracts
- Reassess Step 1 criteria when facts change — especially collectibility
