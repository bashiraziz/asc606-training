# Module 4 — Step 2: Identify performance obligations

## What is a performance obligation?

A performance obligation is a **promise in a contract to transfer a distinct good or service** (or a series of distinct goods or services that are substantially the same) to a customer (ASC 606-10-25-14).

Promises can be:
- **Explicit** — written or stated in the contract
- **Implicit** — based on customary business practices, published policies, or specific statements that create a reasonable customer expectation

:::note Look beyond the written contract
If your standard practice includes free setup, onboarding, or a software update cycle that customers rely on, those implicit promises may be performance obligations even if the contract is silent.
:::

## The distinct test — two-part analysis

A good or service is a **distinct performance obligation** if and only if it satisfies **both** of the following tests:

### Test 1 — Capable of being distinct (ASC 606-10-25-19(a))

The customer can benefit from the good or service either:
- On its own, or
- Together with other resources that are readily available to the customer

"Readily available" means goods or services that the customer can obtain separately — from the entity or from another source.

**Examples of capable of being distinct:**
- A software license (customer can use it with their own servers)
- Professional training (customer can apply it independently)
- A manufactured component (customer can install it themselves)

**Examples of NOT capable of being distinct:**
- A proprietary ingredient that only works with a system the entity also supplies
- Installation of highly specialized equipment where the installer's expertise is embedded in the output

### Test 2 — Distinct within the contract (ASC 606-10-25-19(b))

The entity's promise to transfer the good or service is **separately identifiable** from the other promises in the contract.

Indicators that a promise is NOT separately identifiable (i.e., should be bundled):
1. The entity provides a significant integration service — combining goods/services into a combined output
2. One good/service significantly modifies or customizes another
3. The goods/services are highly interdependent or interrelated

:::tip The bundling question
Ask: "If I removed this element from the contract, would the customer still receive substantially what they bargained for on the other elements?" If yes → separately identifiable. If no → bundle.
:::

## Applying the distinct test — examples

| Scenario | Capable? | Distinct in contract? | PO? |
|---|---|---|---|
| SaaS subscription | Yes — runs independently | Yes — separable from onboarding | Separate PO |
| SaaS onboarding (generic training) | Yes — applicable to other tools | Yes — customer can skip | Separate PO |
| SaaS onboarding (highly customized integration) | Maybe | No — inseparable from SaaS | Bundle with SaaS |
| Software license + PCS (maintenance) | License: Yes | License: Yes / PCS: Yes | Two POs |
| Long-term construction contract | No — work is integrated | No — one combined output | Single PO |
| Equipment + installation (complex, interrelated) | Equipment: Yes | No — entity integrates them | Single PO |

## The series provision (ASC 606-10-25-14(b))

A series of distinct goods or services that are **substantially the same** and have the **same pattern of transfer** to the customer is treated as a **single performance obligation**.

Conditions:
1. Each good/service in the series would be a PO on its own (passes the distinct test)
2. Each good/service has the same pattern of transfer (both over-time, or both point-in-time, using the same method)

**Example:** A monthly cleaning service — each month's service is distinct, but they form a series treated as one PO recognized over time.

**Why it matters:** Simplifies accounting for recurring service contracts by allowing one PO and one measurement method rather than tracking hundreds of individual obligations.

## Principal vs. agent (ASC 606-10-25-25)

When another party is involved in providing goods or services to the customer, determine whether the entity is the **principal** (promises to provide the good/service itself) or an **agent** (arranges for another party to provide).

| | Principal | Agent |
|---|---|---|
| **Revenue** | Gross (full transaction price) | Net (fee/commission only) |
| **Indicator** | Controls the good/service before it is transferred to the customer | Does not control before transfer |

**Control indicators for principal:**
- Responsible if the product is defective or the service fails
- Has inventory risk before customer order or after return rights
- Can direct the use of the good/service before transfer
- Has discretion in establishing prices

:::warning Software marketplaces and distribution
Distributors and resellers who take inventory risk and can set prices are typically principals. App stores and marketplaces that act as agents earn only the platform fee — not gross revenue.
:::

## Material rights (customer options)

A customer option to acquire additional goods or services at a discount is a performance obligation if it provides a **material right** — i.e., a discount the customer would not receive without the contract.

If material, allocate a portion of the transaction price to the option. Recognize that amount when the option is exercised or expires.

**Example:** A loyalty points program — points represent a material right and are a separate PO.

## Bundling vs. splitting — practical summary

```
For each promised good or service in the contract:
  → Apply two-part distinct test
  → If distinct: separate performance obligation
  → If not distinct: combine with related promises until the bundle is distinct
  → Apply series provision if multiple distinct goods/services are substantially same + same transfer pattern
```

## Key takeaways

- A performance obligation exists for every **distinct** promised good or service — not just explicit contractual deliverables
- The distinct test is two-part: capable of being distinct AND distinct within the contract — both must be met
- Bundling occurs when goods/services are so interrelated that they cannot be separately identifiable
- The series provision allows recurring services to be treated as one PO — simplifying measurement
- Principal vs. agent determines whether revenue is recognized gross or net — control before transfer is the decisive factor
- Customer options that are material rights are separate POs — allocate transaction price accordingly
