# Module 2 — Five-step model overview

## The core principle

> *"An entity shall recognize revenue to depict the transfer of promised goods or services to customers in an amount that reflects the consideration to which the entity expects to be entitled in exchange for those goods or services."*
>
> — ASC 606-10-10-2

Everything in ASC 606 flows from this single principle. The five-step model is the structured framework for applying it.

## The five steps at a glance

```
Step 1 ─── Identify the contract
              ↓
Step 2 ─── Identify the performance obligations
              ↓
Step 3 ─── Determine the transaction price
              ↓
Step 4 ─── Allocate the transaction price
              ↓
Step 5 ─── Recognize revenue (as/when POs are satisfied)
```

## Step 1 — Identify the contract (ASC 606-10-25-1)

A contract exists when all five of the following criteria are met:

| Criterion | Description |
|---|---|
| (a) Approval | All parties approved the contract and are committed to perform |
| (b) Rights | Each party's rights regarding the goods or services are identifiable |
| (c) Payment terms | Payment terms are identifiable |
| (d) Commercial substance | The contract has commercial substance |
| (e) Collectibility | It is probable the entity will collect substantially all of the consideration |

If any criterion is not met, you cannot account for the arrangement as a revenue contract. Consideration received is recognized as a liability until criteria are met or the contract is terminated.

**Scope also covers:** contract modifications (3 accounting scenarios), contract combinations, and reassessment when facts change.

## Step 2 — Identify performance obligations (ASC 606-10-25-14)

A performance obligation is a promise to transfer a **distinct** good or service to the customer.

A good or service is distinct if it meets **both** tests:

1. **Capable of being distinct** — The customer can benefit from the good/service on its own or together with other readily available resources
2. **Distinct within the contract** — The promise to transfer the good/service is separately identifiable from other promises in the contract

Goods and services that are not distinct are bundled together into a single performance obligation.

**Also covered:** the series provision, principal vs. agent analysis, material rights, and customer options.

## Step 3 — Determine the transaction price (ASC 606-10-32-2)

The transaction price is the amount of consideration to which the entity expects to be entitled in exchange for transferring goods or services.

Key components:

| Component | Notes |
|---|---|
| Fixed consideration | Base contract price |
| Variable consideration | Estimate using expected value or most likely amount; subject to constraint |
| Significant financing component | Adjust for time value when payment timing is more than 12 months from performance |
| Non-cash consideration | Measure at fair value |
| Consideration payable to customer | Reduce transaction price (unless for distinct good/service) |

## Step 4 — Allocate the transaction price (ASC 606-10-32-28)

The transaction price is allocated to performance obligations **in proportion to their relative stand-alone selling prices (SSPs)**.

When stand-alone selling prices are not directly observable, estimate them using:

1. **Adjusted market assessment approach** — Evaluate market price for similar goods/services
2. **Expected cost plus margin approach** — Forecast costs and add an appropriate margin
3. **Residual approach** — Only permitted when SSP is highly variable or uncertain

Discounts and variable consideration may be allocated entirely to one or more (but not all) performance obligations when specific criteria are met.

## Step 5 — Recognize revenue (ASC 606-10-25-23)

Revenue is recognized **when (or as) a performance obligation is satisfied** — that is, when control of the promised good or service transfers to the customer.

**Over time** — recognize as performance occurs if any one of three criteria is met:
1. The customer simultaneously receives and consumes the benefits
2. The entity's performance creates or enhances an asset the customer controls
3. The entity's performance does not create an asset with an alternative use, and the entity has an enforceable right to payment for performance to date

**Point in time** — recognize when control transfers (typically on delivery, acceptance, or title transfer).

For over-time recognition, an appropriate **progress measurement method** must be selected (input or output method) and applied consistently.

## How the steps interact

The steps are sequential in analysis but **not always linear in practice**:

:::warning Revisiting earlier steps
A **contract modification** (Step 1) may require re-identification of performance obligations (Step 2) and reallocation of the transaction price (Step 4).

A change in **variable consideration estimates** (Step 3) requires catching up cumulative revenue in the current period — not restating prior periods (cumulative catch-up constraint).

**Collectibility reassessment** (Step 1, criterion e) is required when facts change — a deteriorating customer credit situation can suspend revenue recognition mid-contract.
:::

## When to apply the five steps

The five-step analysis applies:

- **At contract inception** — before any revenue is recognized
- **When a modification occurs** — reassess steps 1–4
- **At each reporting date** — reassess variable consideration (Step 3) and progress measurement (Step 5)
- **When a triggering event occurs** — significant change in facts affecting any step

## Key takeaways

- The five-step model is the universal framework for all customer contracts under ASC 606
- Step 2 (performance obligations) is often the most judgment-intensive — getting it wrong cascades into Steps 4 and 5
- Steps are sequential in structure but require reassessment when contract facts change
- The core question at every step is: *has control transferred, to what extent, and for what amount?*
- For multi-element arrangements, the allocation in Step 4 determines how much revenue each obligation earns — getting SSPs right is critical
