# Module 1 — Introduction & background

## Why revenue recognition matters

Revenue is the top line of every income statement — and for most companies, the single largest number that investors, lenders, and regulators scrutinize. Getting it wrong has consequences:

- **Financial restatements** that destroy credibility and trigger SEC enforcement
- **Mispriced equity** when investors can't trust reported numbers
- **Audit failures** when auditors miss premature or deferred recognition
- **Contract pricing errors** when sales teams don't understand how deals will be booked

ASC 606 is the current GAAP standard for virtually all revenue from customer contracts. Understanding it is not optional for anyone in finance, accounting, audit, or FP&A.

## The history: from SAB 104 to ASC 606

Before ASC 606, revenue recognition guidance was fragmented across dozens of industry-specific rules and SEC staff bulletins — particularly **SAB 104** (Staff Accounting Bulletin 104) and **SOP 97-2** (AICPA Statement of Position 97-2 for software).

### Problems with the old framework

- Different rules for software, construction, real estate, and services created inconsistency
- Companies in the same industry could recognize revenue differently for identical transactions
- Rules were often too bright-line — they prescribed *when* rather than *whether* control had transferred
- Financial statement users could not easily compare revenue across industries

### The convergence project

In 2002, FASB and IASB began a joint project to develop a single, principle-based revenue standard. The result was:

| Standard | Jurisdiction | Effective date |
|---|---|---|
| ASC 606 | U.S. GAAP (FASB) | Annual periods beginning after Dec 15, 2017 (public); Dec 15, 2018 (nonpublic) |
| IFRS 15 | IFRS (IASB) | Jan 1, 2018 |

The two standards are largely converged — the five-step model is identical. Differences exist in certain transition options and narrow scoping rules.

### What changed

ASC 606 replaced nearly all of the old guidance — including SAB 104, SOP 97-2, and dozens of industry-specific rules — with a single, principle-based framework built around the concept of **control transfer**.

:::tip The core shift
Old GAAP: *When are risks and rewards transferred?*

ASC 606: *When does control of the promised good or service transfer to the customer?*

This shift matters because it forces you to analyze the economics of each transaction rather than following a checklist.
:::

## Scope

### What ASC 606 covers

ASC 606 applies to **revenue from contracts with customers** — any agreement in which an entity undertakes to transfer goods or services to a customer in exchange for consideration.

### Scope exclusions — what ASC 606 does NOT cover

The following are excluded from ASC 606 and governed by other standards:

| Excluded item | Governing standard |
|---|---|
| Lease contracts | ASC 842 (Leases) |
| Insurance contracts | ASC 944 (Insurance) |
| Financial instruments | ASC 815/825 (derivatives, investments) |
| Guarantees | ASC 460 (Guarantees) |
| Non-monetary exchanges between entities in the same industry | ASC 845 |
| Certain collaborative arrangements | ASC 808 |

:::warning Partial scope
A single contract may have elements subject to ASC 606 and elements subject to other standards. When this occurs, separate the components and apply the appropriate standard to each part.
:::

## The five-step model — a preview

ASC 606 establishes a single revenue recognition framework built around five steps:

1. **Identify the contract** with the customer
2. **Identify the performance obligations** in the contract
3. **Determine the transaction price**
4. **Allocate the transaction price** to the performance obligations
5. **Recognize revenue** when (or as) each performance obligation is satisfied

The entire training manual follows this structure. Each subsequent module covers one step in depth.

:::note
Steps are not always linear. A modification in Step 1, for example, can require you to re-evaluate performance obligations in Step 2 and re-allocate in Step 4.
:::

## Key takeaways

- ASC 606 replaced fragmented U.S. GAAP revenue guidance (SAB 104, SOP 97-2, and dozens of industry rules) effective for most entities in 2018
- The core principle is **control transfer** — revenue is recognized when control of a promised good or service passes to the customer
- The standard applies to virtually all customer contracts; exclusions include leases, insurance, and financial instruments
- A five-step model provides the analytical framework — all five steps must be applied to every contract
- ASC 606 and IFRS 15 are substantially converged; the five-step model is identical under both standards
