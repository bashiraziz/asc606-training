# Module 5 — Step 3: Determine the transaction price

## Definition

The transaction price is the amount of consideration to which the entity **expects to be entitled** in exchange for transferring promised goods or services to a customer, excluding amounts collected on behalf of third parties (e.g., sales taxes) (ASC 606-10-32-2).

Key word: *expects* — not the stated contract price. If you routinely accept less, estimate what you will actually collect.

## Components of the transaction price

The transaction price may include up to five components:

1. Fixed consideration
2. Variable consideration (net of constraint)
3. Significant financing component adjustment
4. Non-cash consideration
5. Consideration payable to customer (reduction)

## 1. Fixed consideration

The contractual amount stated as a fixed price. This is the baseline.

## 2. Variable consideration (ASC 606-10-32-5)

Variable consideration includes any amount that varies based on future outcomes — discounts, rebates, refunds, credits, price concessions, incentives, performance bonuses, or penalties.

### Estimation methods

Two methods are permitted; choose the one that **better predicts** the amount to which the entity will be entitled:

| Method | When to use | How it works |
|---|---|---|
| **Expected value (EV)** | Wide range of possible outcomes; large volume of similar contracts | Probability-weighted average of all possible amounts |
| **Most likely amount (MLA)** | Binary or limited outcomes (e.g., bonus is either earned or not) | The single most likely outcome in the range |

:::tip Choosing a method
Use the method that best predicts the outcome. For a performance bonus that is either $0 or $500,000, MLA is better — the EV would give a mid-point that is never actually paid. For a customer rebate based on tiered volume, EV is better because many volume levels are possible.
:::

### The variable consideration constraint (ASC 606-10-32-11)

Include variable consideration only to the extent it is **highly probable** that a significant revenue reversal will not occur when the uncertainty is resolved.

Factors that increase the risk of reversal (apply more constraint):
- Highly susceptible to factors outside the entity's control
- Long time before uncertainty resolved
- Limited experience with similar contracts
- Wide range of possible outcomes
- Practice of offering price concessions

**Three constraint levels:**

| Level | Treatment |
|---|---|
| No constraint | Include full estimated VC in transaction price |
| Partially constrained | Include only the amount highly probable of no reversal |
| Fully constrained | Exclude VC entirely until uncertainty resolves |

:::warning Reassessment at every reporting date
Variable consideration estimates and constraint determinations must be **updated at each reporting date**. Cumulative catch-up adjustments are recorded in the current period — not as restatements.
:::

## 3. Significant financing component (ASC 606-10-32-15)

If the timing of payments provides the customer or the entity with a significant benefit of financing, adjust the transaction price for the time value of money.

**Indicators of a significant financing component:**
- Significant difference between cash selling price and stated contract price
- Payment timing is materially different from when control transfers (more than 12 months)
- Market rate of interest is materially different from the contract rate

**Practical expedient:** An entity need not adjust for the financing component if the period between payment and transfer of goods/services is **one year or less** at contract inception (ASC 606-10-32-18).

### Direction of adjustment

| Payment timing | Effect on TP |
|---|---|
| Customer pays **before** performance | Interest **added** (increase TP; recognize interest expense) |
| Customer pays **after** performance | Interest **subtracted** (decrease TP; recognize interest income) |

## 4. Non-cash consideration (ASC 606-10-32-21)

When a customer pays with goods, services, or other non-cash assets, measure the transaction price at the **fair value** of the non-cash consideration at contract inception.

If fair value cannot be reasonably estimated, measure it by reference to the standalone selling price of the goods or services promised.

## 5. Consideration payable to customer (ASC 606-10-32-25)

Payments from the entity to the customer — including cash, credits, coupons, vouchers, or other items — reduce the transaction price unless they are in exchange for a **distinct good or service** from the customer.

If the payment is for a distinct good or service received: treat as a purchase (expense or asset).
If not: reduce transaction price → reduce revenue.

:::warning Channel fees
Slotting fees, cooperative advertising payments, and similar payments to resellers/distributors are typically reductions of TP, not marketing expenses — unless the entity receives a distinct benefit.
:::

## Calculating the transaction price — formula

```
Transaction price =
  Base fixed price
  + Variable consideration (net of constraint)
  ± Significant financing component adjustment
  + Non-cash consideration (at FV)
  − Consideration payable to customer (if not for distinct good/service)
```

## Example — SaaS contract with bonus

A SaaS company signs a 12-month contract for $120,000 base fee. If uptime exceeds 99.9%, a $15,000 bonus is paid.

**Facts:** Based on history, there is a 70% probability of achieving the uptime target.

**EV approach:** $15,000 × 70% = $10,500 estimated VC.

**Constraint check:** Is it highly probable no significant reversal occurs? Uptime is largely in the entity's control and history supports 70% achievement. Assume no constraint. Include $10,500.

**Transaction price:** $120,000 + $10,500 = **$130,500**

## Key takeaways

- The transaction price is what the entity *expects* to collect — not always the stated contract price
- Variable consideration is estimated using EV or MLA, then constrained to the amount highly probable of no reversal
- Constraint is assessed at every reporting date — update estimates and record catch-up adjustments as facts change
- Significant financing adjustments apply when payments are more than 12 months from performance; a practical expedient exempts contracts ≤12 months
- Payments to customers reduce the transaction price unless in exchange for a distinct good or service
