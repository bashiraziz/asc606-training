# Module 6 — Step 4: Allocate the transaction price

## The allocation objective

When a contract has more than one performance obligation, the transaction price must be allocated to each PO in a way that depicts the amount of consideration the entity **expects to be entitled to** in exchange for satisfying each obligation (ASC 606-10-32-28).

The allocation is based on **relative stand-alone selling prices (SSPs)**.

## Stand-alone selling price (SSP)

The SSP is the price at which the entity would sell the good or service **separately to a customer** — i.e., not as part of a bundle.

### Best evidence: observable prices

The best evidence of SSP is the price the entity charges when it sells the good or service standalone. If the entity regularly sells items separately, use those prices.

### When SSP is not directly observable — estimation methods

Three methods are permitted (ASC 606-10-32-34):

| Method | Description | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Adjusted market assessment** | Evaluate the market and estimate what a customer would pay | Comparable observable market prices exist |
| **Expected cost plus margin** | Estimate expected costs and add an appropriate margin | Costs are estimable; margin is supportable |
| **Residual approach** | SSP = Total TP − sum of SSPs of other POs | Only when SSP is **highly variable** or **uncertain** (narrow use) |

:::warning Residual approach limitations
The residual approach can only be used when the SSP of a good or service is highly variable (sold at widely varying prices) or uncertain (no historical selling price). It cannot be used simply because it is convenient.
:::

## Proportional allocation — the default

The default method allocates the transaction price in proportion to relative SSPs:

```
Allocated TP for PO₁ = (SSP₁ / Total SSP) × Transaction Price
```

**Example:**

| PO | SSP | SSP % | Allocated TP |
|---|---|---|---|
| SaaS license | $100,000 | 62.5% | $62,500 |
| Onboarding services | $40,000 | 25.0% | $25,000 |
| Training | $20,000 | 12.5% | $12,500 |
| **Total** | **$160,000** | **100%** | **$100,000** |

Note: the total transaction price ($100,000) is less than the total SSP ($160,000), meaning the bundle is offered at a $60,000 discount. That discount is absorbed proportionally across all POs.

## Allocating discounts (ASC 606-10-32-36)

Discounts are generally allocated proportionally. However, a discount may be **allocated entirely to one or more (but not all) POs** if all three criteria are met:

1. The entity regularly sells each PO in the group separately
2. The entity also regularly sells one subset of those POs as a bundle at a discount
3. The discount associated with that bundle is substantially the same as the discount in the contract

:::tip Why this matters
If an entity bundles software + installation at a fixed price but charges full price for software alone, the discount arguably belongs entirely to the installation — not spread across both. This exception allows that allocation.
:::

## Allocating variable consideration (ASC 606-10-32-39)

Variable consideration should also be allocated proportionally. However, variable consideration may be allocated **entirely to one PO** if both conditions are met:

1. The variable consideration relates specifically to satisfying that PO (or a distinct good/service within a series)
2. Allocating the variable amount entirely to that PO is consistent with the overall allocation objective

**Example:** A performance bonus payable only if a specific software module goes live on time relates entirely to the delivery of that module — not to unrelated maintenance services in the same contract.

## Changes in transaction price after contract inception

When the transaction price changes after contract inception (e.g., due to VC reassessment), the change is allocated using the **same method as at inception** — proportional to SSPs.

The cumulative catch-up adjustment is recognized in the current period for POs already partially or fully satisfied.

## SSP estimation in practice

Establishing defensible SSPs requires:

1. A consistent methodology applied to all similar contracts
2. Documentation of the basis for each SSP
3. Regular updates when market conditions change
4. Auditor-supportable evidence — price lists, historical sales data, market studies

:::warning Don't reverse-engineer SSPs
SSPs should be established **before** the contract is signed, not after. Back-calculating an SSP to achieve a desired revenue result violates the allocation objective and creates audit risk.
:::

## Key takeaways

- Allocation is based on relative SSPs — proportional by default
- SSP best evidence is observable prices; when not observable, use adjusted market assessment, expected cost plus margin, or (only in narrow circumstances) residual approach
- Discounts may be allocated entirely to a subset of POs when specific bundling evidence exists
- Variable consideration may be allocated to a single PO when it specifically relates to that obligation
- SSP methodologies must be consistent, documented, and updated — not reverse-engineered after the fact
